Extreme Weather Threatens Malaysia’s Food Security

Agriculture remains central to Malaysia’s economy and identity, yet climate change is placing the sector under increasing strain. Rising temperatures, erratic rainfall, floods and prolonged droughts are disrupting food production and distribution, weakening supply chains nationwide and reducing overall agricultural productivity and long-term stability.

Recent severe flooding across Johor, Melaka, Perak, Kelantan and Terengganu have destroyed over 100 hectares of farmland, causing vegetable prices to surge by 50–80%. Prices of common vegetables such as sawi, bayam and kangkung rose from RM3.50 to RM8.00 per kilogram, while red peppers increased to RM22.00. Overall, food prices recorded a 2.6% year-on-year increase, highlighting how climate-related disruptions are straining Malaysia’s food supply chain and limiting access to nutritious food for households nationwide.

To safeguard food security, Malaysia must invest in climate-adaptive farming practices, including flood-tolerant crops, precision agriculture and early warning systems to protect production from increasingly extreme weather patterns. Coordinated efforts across government, farmers and communities are essential to build a more resilient and sustainable food system. Addressing climate change is no longer optional — it is vital to securing Malaysia’s food future.
