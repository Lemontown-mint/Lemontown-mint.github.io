Finger Counting Improves Children’s Math Learning

A long-term study suggests that counting on fingers in primary school is not a weakness but a valuable tool for learning mathematics. Researchers found that children who use their fingers to solve arithmetic problems tend to develop stronger calculation skills over time. Parents and teachers are encouraged not to discourage this habit, as it supports mathematical understanding.

The study followed around 200 children aged four-and-a-half to seven-and-a-half, observing their addition skills over several years without giving specific finger-counting instruction. The findings showed that children who began using their fingers earlier were more likely to become high performers in arithmetic later on. In contrast, those who never used their fingers consistently showed the weakest results.

The researchers explain that while children who stop finger counting around age seven often perform better at that stage, this is because they previously learned with their hands and later transitioned to mental calculation. Former finger counters ultimately outperformed both persistent finger users and those who never relied on their hands. Even if older children still use their fingers, researchers advise against criticism, as it may still support their development.
