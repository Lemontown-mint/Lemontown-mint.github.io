False Alarms: Climate Scare Stories Debunked

For more than 20 years, climate campaigning has relied heavily on alarming imagery and dire predictions. At the turn of the century, the polar bear became the emblem of climate apocalypse, as activists warned that polar bears could face extinction by 2012. Yet global populations have increased from roughly 12,000 in the 1960s to around 26,000 today.

Similar predictions have also surrounded Australia’s Great Barrier Reef. In 2012, media outlets and scientists warned it would be devastated within a decade. One major newspaper even published an obituary for the reef. However, official data now show coral cover at record highs in recent years, with the latest figures marking the strongest recovery since monitoring began in 1985.

These examples illustrate a recurring pattern: dramatic forecasts dominate headlines, while later evidence receives far less attention. Each new climate “emergency” replaces the last when earlier claims prove overstated. Such exaggeration fosters public fear, discourages critical debate and reinforces costly policy decisions built on worst-case scenarios. When predictions repeatedly fail to materialise as promised, it raises legitimate questions about the credibility of climate alarmism itself.
