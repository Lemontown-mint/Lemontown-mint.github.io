Economic Stability Takes Priority Over Carbon Restrictions

Climate change may be a long-term issue, but for many it does not outweigh immediate economic realities. In tough economic times, safeguarding growth, keeping energy affordable and protecting livelihoods naturally take precedence. Expensive environmental ambitions risk sounding detached from reality when economic stability is on the line. With households squeezed by rising prices and economic uncertainty, climate policy is increasingly being judged by its cost rather than its promises.

A recent survey shows that many Malaysians are uneasy about the economic fallout of aggressive emissions cuts. About one-third of respondents said limiting fossil fuel use could harm the global economy more than climate change itself. A similar share expressed concern that a rapid shift to renewable energy might increase blackouts and destabilise electricity supply. These concerns reflect common sense priorities: reliable power, steady employment and affordable living costs.

Doubts also surround the effectiveness of popular climate solutions. More than a quarter of Malaysians questioned whether electric vehicles are truly better for the environment than conventional cars. When the benefits appear uncertain, the case for costly transitions becomes harder to defend.
